Research mission

In short

The overarching philosphy of my work is this: by learning from the massive amounts of software-related data out there, we can remove the redundancies and complexities of software development and make software engineering (rather than development) accessible to all. Here, I believe, lies the solution to the software crisis.

In full

The primary focus of my research is to simplify software development for the masses. Software Engineering is key here, as it adds discipline and rigour to the far-from-simple task of creating "good" software. In practice, however, software engineering remains a fuzzy concept. Questions that are fundamental to an engineering discipline ("what defines `good code'?"; "how should design and architecture be included in software development?") remain only partially answered and differently so in many domains.

I believe that, to answer questions such as the above, we must study code in the real world. Thus, I focus on Mining Software Repositories and Empirical Software Engineering. I look for code that development communities find natural (and the reverse) and study its properties. These properties can then be turned into tools to make developing sound software easier. I investigate how people collaborate, focussing on how we can support maintainers and contributors alike in order improve the evolution of source code.

Other research

In my spare-time, I try to regularly take on projects that are either "just-off" my main research or completely out there. This helps me relax and broaden my horizons. Current topics of interest are (Deep) Neural Networks, Optimization Problems and the (cursed) Collatz Conjecture.
